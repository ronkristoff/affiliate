Module 1

What Is Affiliate Marketing — And Why Does It Exist?

Before we look at any code, let's understand the problem this entire system was built to solve.

The Problem Every SaaS Owner Faces

Imagine you run a software company — let's call it TechFlow. You've built a great product, but nobody knows about it. You need customers, and the traditional ways to get them are expensive:

📺

Ads

You pay upfront whether anyone buys or not. Risky.

📧

Email Campaigns

People ignore them. Open rates are dropping every year.

🤝

Affiliate Marketing

You only pay when someone actually brings you a paying customer. Zero risk.

💡 Think of it like this

Affiliate marketing is like having a network of freelance salespeople who only get paid when they close a deal. No salary, no benefits — just a commission on each sale they bring in. It's the ultimate performance-based marketing model.

The Core Concept in One Sentence

Affiliate marketing is when a person (the affiliate) promotes a company's product using a special tracking link. When someone clicks that link and buys the product, the affiliate earns a cut of the sale.

1

Affiliate gets a unique tracking link

Like yourproduct.com?ref=jamie123 — this link identifies which affiliate sent the customer.

2

Affiliate shares the link with their audience

On their blog, YouTube channel, social media, or newsletter.

3

A customer clicks and buys

The system records the click, tracks the purchase, and attributes it to the affiliate.

4

Affiliate earns a commission

A percentage or fixed amount of the sale goes into the affiliate's account.

Why Build an Entire Platform for This?

You might wonder: "Can't this just be a spreadsheet?" For 2 affiliates, maybe. But when you have dozens of affiliates, multiple campaigns, recurring subscriptions, fraud detection, and payout batches — you need a real system.

🔗

Tracking

Who clicked what, when, and from where? Every click needs to be recorded and deduplicated.

💰

Commission Calculation

Different campaigns pay differently — percentages, flat fees, recurring rates. The math must be exact.

🛡️

Fraud Prevention

What if an affiliate tries to earn commissions on their own purchases? The system must detect and flag this.

📦

Payout Management

Grouping commissions into batches, tracking which affiliates have been paid, and generating payout reports.

💡
Aha! Moment

This entire codebase — hundreds of files — exists to automate what would otherwise be a nightmare of spreadsheets, manual calculations, and trust issues between SaaS owners and their affiliates.

Every affiliate system has four key players. Understanding who they are and what they do is essential to understanding how the code works.

The Four Key Players

🏢

The SaaS Owner ("Alex")

📣

A content creator, blogger, or marketer. Promotes the SaaS product to their audience. Earns a commission for every customer they bring in. Needs clear dashboards and transparent tracking.

🛒

⚙️

The Platform (This Codebase)

The automated system that connects everyone. Tracks clicks, calculates commissions, detects fraud, processes payouts, and keeps all the data organized and accurate.

One Platform, Many Companies

Here's what makes this system special: it's multi-tenant. That means one installation of this software can serve many different SaaS companies simultaneously, and each company's data is completely isolated from the others.

💡 Think of it like this

Imagine an office building where each company has its own locked floor. They all share the same building (the platform), but TechFlow can't see what's happening on GHL Agency Pro's floor, and vice versa. Every piece of data — every affiliate, every commission — is tagged with a tenantId that keeps it in the right company's "floor."

Stage 1: The Click — "Someone Followed the Link"

When a potential customer clicks an affiliate's tracking link, a carefully orchestrated chain of events kicks off. This is the first link in the attribution chain — and it's more sophisticated than you might think.

💡 Think of it like this

Imagine a coat-check attendant at a fancy event. When you hand over your coat, they give you a numbered ticket. Later, you show that ticket to get your coat back. The click tracking system does the same thing — it gives the customer a digital "ticket" (a cookie) that proves which affiliate sent them. No ticket, no commission.

How the Tracking Link Works — Step by Step

Affiliate referral links come in three formats:

🔗 Short Link

techflow.com/ref/JAMIE8X2

The default format. The 8-character code maps to the affiliate's default campaign.

🎯 Campaign-Specific

techflow.com/ref/JAMIE8X2?campaign=summer-sale

Routes to a specific campaign by slug. Useful when an affiliate promotes different offers.

✨ Vanity URL

techflow.com/ref/jamie-tech-reviews

A memorable, branded slug. Much better for sharing on social media and YouTube.

📋
Character Set Design

The 8-character codes use ABCDEFGHJKLMNPQRSTUVWXYZ23456789 — deliberately excluding 0, O, I, and 1 to avoid confusion. Nobody can mistake a zero for the letter O when they're reading the code from a YouTube description.

What Happens When Someone Clicks

Here's the actual technical flow, from the browser to the database:

1

track.js intercepts the URL

The merchant has installed a JavaScript snippet (track.js) on their website. When a visitor lands on /ref/JAMIE8X2, track.js detects the path, extracts the referral code, and calls the tracking API.

2

Cookie precedence check

Before processing the new code, track.js reads any existing _affilio cookie. If the cookie already has a valid, active affiliate, the existing cookie takes priority. This implements a "last active affiliate wins within the window" policy — a new click can't steal credit from an affiliate whose cookie is still fresh.

3

API call: GET /track/click?code=JAMIE8X2&t=tenantId

track.js sends the click to the server with the referral code and tenant ID. The server extracts the visitor's IP address (from X-Forwarded-For header), user agent, and referrer.

4

Server validates the affiliate

The server looks up the referral code in the database. If the affiliate is suspended or pending, the link returns a 404 — dead links for dead accounts. If the campaign is archived, also 404. If paused, returns 503 with an explanation.

5

Deduplication check

The server generates a dedupeKey in the format {IP_address}:{code}:{hourlyTimeBucket}. If a click with this key already exists in the database, it returns the existing click record instead of creating a new one. This means the same person clicking the same link multiple times within the same hour only counts as one click.

6

Click is recorded (or returned)

A new click record is inserted into the database with: tenant ID, referral link ID, affiliate ID, campaign ID, IP address, user agent, referrer, and the deduplication key.

7

The _affilio cookie is set

The server returns attribution data (affiliate code, click ID, tenant ID, campaign ID, cookie duration in days). track.js stores this as a base64-encoded JSON cookie called _affilio. The cookie's expiration matches the campaign's cookieDuration (default: 30 days).

8

URL is cleaned up

track.js uses history.replaceState() to remove the /ref/... path from the browser's URL bar. The customer sees a clean URL (techflow.com) — they may never know they came through an affiliate link.

// convex/http.ts — GET /track/click handler
// (simplified for clarity)

// 1. Generate dedupeKey
const dedupeKey =
  `${clientIp}:${code}:${hourlyTimeBucket}`;

// 2. Check cookie precedence
const existingCookie = parseCookie(
  req.headers.get("Cookie")
);
if (existingCookie?.sa_aff?.affiliateCode) {
  // Existing cookie takes priority
  code = existingCookie.sa_aff.affiliateCode;
}

// 3. Validate affiliate is active
if (affiliate.status !== "active") {
  return new Response("Not found",
    { status: 404 });
}

// 4. Record click via internal mutation
const result = await ctx.runMutation(
  internal.clicks.trackClickInternal, {
    tenantId, referralLinkId,
    affiliateId, campaignId,
    ipAddress: clientIp,
    dedupeKey, userAgent, referrer,
  }
);
Build a deduplication key from the visitor's IP, the referral code, and the current hour. Same person + same link + same hour = same key = one click.
Check existing cookie first — if the visitor already has an active affiliate cookie, use that affiliate instead. This prevents new clicks from hijacking credit.
Validate the affiliate — suspended or pending affiliates get a 404. Their links are dead.
Record the click — call the internal mutation to insert (or return existing) click record with all tracking data.
// public/track.js — Cookie setting (client-side)

// Server returns JSON with attribution data
// { affiliateCode, clickId, tenantId,
//   campaignId, cookieDurationDays }

// Store as base64-encoded JSON cookie
const cookieData = JSON.stringify({
  code: data.affiliateCode,
  clickId: data.clickId,
  tenantId: data.tenantId,
  timestamp: Date.now(),
});

document.cookie =
  `_affilio=${btoa(cookieData)}; ` +
  `max-age=${days * 86400}; path=/`;

// Clean the URL bar
history.replaceState(
  {}, "", "/"
);
Encode the attribution data as JSON and base64-encode it into a cookie called _affilio. This cookie travels with every future request from this browser.
Set the cookie expiration to match the campaign's cookie duration (default 30 days). After that, the cookie dies and no more attribution.
Clean the URL so the customer doesn't see the ugly /ref/JAMIE8X2 in their address bar. Seamless experience.
💡
Why hourly deduplication?

The deduplication key uses an hourly time bucket, not a daily one. This means if the same person clicks the same link at 2:00 PM and 3:30 PM, those are two different hours — so two clicks are recorded. But if they click at 2:00 PM and 2:45 PM, only one click is recorded. This gives you more granular tracking while still preventing page-refresh inflation.

Why lead capture exists

Not every payment system supports passing affiliate metadata through webhooks. Stripe Connect, for example, doesn't carry an _affilio_ref field in its events. The lead capture system solves this: when the customer enters their email during signup (while the affiliate cookie is still active), the system records "this email was referred by Jamie." Later, when Stripe sends a checkout.session.completed webhook with the customer's email, the system matches it to the lead — and Jamie gets credit. This is how universal payment provider support works.

Stage 2: The Conversion — "They Actually Bought Something"

A click means nothing if the person doesn't buy. A conversion is when a click results in a paying customer. This is where the money happens — and the system has four different paths to detect and attribute conversions.

The Four Attribution Sources

🍪

Cookie Attribution

The customer had the _affilio tracking cookie in their browser. When the merchant calls the conversion API, the system reads the cookie and credits the affiliate. Works with any payment provider — as long as the merchant calls POST /track/conversion.

📝

Body Attribution

The merchant sends the affiliate code and click ID directly in the API request body. This takes priority over cookies — useful when the merchant wants to control attribution explicitly (e.g., server-side checkout flows where cookies aren't available).

🔔

Webhook Attribution

The payment system sends a webhook with affiliate metadata embedded in the payload. Some providers, like SaligPay, include fields such as _affilio_ref in their events. No cookies or API calls needed — attribution comes directly from the payment provider.

📧

Lead Matching (Email Attribution)

The customer was captured as a "referral lead" when they entered their email during signup (via Affilio.referral({email})). When a payment webhook arrives with that email but no direct affiliate metadata, the system matches the email to the lead. This is how Stripe integration works — Stripe webhooks don't carry affiliate metadata.

📋
Attribution Priority Order

When a conversion is created via POST /track/conversion, the system checks in this order: (1) Body params (explicit code/clickId) → (2) Cookie (_affilio) → (3) No attribution → organic conversion. When a conversion is created via webhook, the order is: (1) Webhook metadata → (2) Lead matching (email lookup) → (3) No attribution → organic.

How the Conversion API Works

The primary conversion endpoint is POST /track/conversion. Here's the full flow:

1

Merchant calls the conversion API

After a successful payment on their site, the merchant (or track.js) sends a POST request with the sale amount, order ID, customer email, and optionally the affiliate code. Authentication uses a X-Tracking-Key header (the tenant's public tracking key).

2

System resolves attribution

First checks for body params (explicit affiliate code). If none, falls back to the _affilio cookie. If no cookie, creates an organic conversion (no commission).

3

Cookie window validation

Even if a cookie exists, the system checks if it's within the campaign's cookieDuration (default 30 days). If the cookie has expired, the conversion is recorded as organic. Expired cookies don't get credit.

4

Click validation

The system looks up the click record referenced by the cookie (or body param). It validates that the click exists, belongs to the right affiliate, and is within the attribution window.

5

Self-referral detection

Before creating the conversion, the system checks if the customer's email matches the affiliate's email (simple check). If self-referral is suspected, the conversion is still recorded but flagged.

6

Conversion record created

A conversion record is inserted with the full attribution chain: tenant, affiliate, referral link, click, campaign, amount, customer email, and attributionSource (one of: "cookie", "body", "webhook", "organic").

// convex/http.ts — POST /track/conversion
// (simplified for clarity)

// 1. Check body for explicit attribution
const bodyCode = body.affiliateCode;
const bodyClickId = body.clickId;

// 2. Fall back to _affilio cookie
let cookieData = null;
if (!bodyCode) {
  const raw = parseCookie(
    req.headers.get("Cookie")
  );
  cookieData = JSON.parse(
    atob(raw._affilio)
  );
}

// 3. No attribution → organic
if (!bodyCode && !cookieData) {
  await ctx.runMutation(
    internal.conversions
      .createConversionInternal, {
      tenantId, amount,
      customerEmail: body.customerEmail,
      attributionSource: "organic",
    }
  );
  return;
}

// 4. Validate cookie window
if (cookieData) {
  const daysSinceClick =
    (Date.now() - cookieData.timestamp)
    / (1000 * 60 * 60 * 24);
  if (daysSinceClick > cookieDurationDays) {
    // Cookie expired → organic
    attributionSource = "organic";
  }
}
Check the request body first — if the merchant sent an explicit affiliate code, use that. Body attribution always takes priority.
Fall back to the cookie — decode the base64 _affilio cookie to get the affiliate code, click ID, tenant ID, and timestamp.
No attribution at all? Record the conversion as "organic" — still tracked for analytics, but no commission is created.
Check the cookie age — if the cookie is older than the campaign's cookie duration (default 30 days), treat it as organic. Time's up.

Lead Matching — The Stripe Bridge

When a payment webhook arrives without any affiliate metadata (common with Stripe), the system falls back to email-based lead matching:

1

Customer clicked affiliate link

Jamie's audience member clicks her link → track.js sets the _affilio cookie.

2

Customer enters email on signup form

The merchant calls Affilio.referral({email: "alex@example.com"}). This sends POST /track/referral, which creates a referral lead record linking alex@example.com → Jamie's affiliate ID → referral link.

3

Customer completes payment via Stripe

Stripe sends a checkout.session.completed webhook with customer_email: "alex@example.com" but NO affiliate metadata.

4

Commission engine matches the email

The commission engine calls resolveLeadAttribution, which looks up the referral leads table for an active lead matching this tenant + email. It finds Jamie's lead and attributes the conversion.

5

Lead status changes to "converted"

The lead is marked as converted. Leads that aren't converted within 90 days expire automatically (via a cron job).

⚠️
First-Affiliate-Wins Policy

If an active referral lead already exists for a tenant + email combination, a new affiliate click does NOT overwrite it. The first affiliate to capture that email keeps the credit. This prevents "last-second hijacking" where a different affiliate tries to steal a customer who's already in someone else's funnel.

💡
Why four attribution sources?

Different payment providers and integration setups require different approaches. SaligPay embeds affiliate metadata directly in webhooks (webhook attribution). Stripe doesn't, so you need lead matching. Custom checkouts might not have cookies, so body attribution is the fallback. And cookie attribution is the universal default that works with any provider when the merchant calls POST /track/conversion. Supporting all four means this platform works with any payment system — not just one.

Stage 3: The Commission — "Here's What You Earned"

Once a conversion is recorded, the system calculates how much the affiliate earns. This is where the commission engine kicks in, and it handles many different scenarios.

1

Resolve attribution

For webhook-driven conversions, the commission engine first looks for affiliate metadata in the webhook payload (_affilio_ref). If none found, it falls back to email-based lead matching via the referralLeads table. If still no match → organic conversion, no commission.

2

Validate payment status

Only payments with status "paid" or "completed" generate commissions. Payments with status "pending" or "failed" are explicitly rejected — a security audit event is logged to catch potential webhook manipulation attempts.

3

Check campaign status

Is the campaign still active? Paused and archived campaigns cannot earn new commissions. This prevents paying commissions for products that are no longer being promoted.

4

Calculate the commission amount

Based on the campaign's commission type: percentage (e.g., 20% of sale) or flat fee (e.g., $50 per sale). For subscription renewals, the recurring commission rate is used instead. If the affiliate has a custom override rate, that takes priority over everything.

5

Run self-referral detection

The fraud detection system compares the customer's email, IP, device fingerprint, and payment method against the affiliate's data using a weighted scoring system. If the score reaches 3+, the commission is flagged.

6

Set commission status

Commissions start as Pending. If the campaign has auto-approval enabled and the amount is below the threshold AND no fraud was detected, it becomes Approved immediately. Fraud-flagged commissions are always forced to pending, regardless of auto-approval settings.

// convex/commissionEngine.ts
// processPaymentUpdatedToCommission (simplified)

// 1. Resolve attribution from webhook or lead match
let affiliateCode =
  event.attribution?.affiliateCode;
if (!affiliateCode) {
  const lead = await ctx.runQuery(
    internal.referralLeads
      .resolveLeadAttribution, {
    tenantId,
    email: event.payment.customerEmail,
  });
  affiliateCode = lead?.affiliateCode;
}

// 2. Validate payment status
if (event.payment.status !== "paid"
  && event.payment.status !== "completed") {
  // Log security audit event, don't create
  return;
}

// 3. Get effective rate (override or default)
const effectiveRate = affiliate
  .commissionOverrides?.find(
    (o) => o.campaignId === campaignId
        && o.status === "active"
  )?.rate ?? campaign.commissionValue;

// 4. Calculate commission
if (campaign.commissionType === "percentage") {
  amount = saleAmount * (effectiveRate / 100);
} else {
  amount = effectiveRate; // flat_fee
}
Look for affiliate metadata in the webhook payload first. If the payment provider didn't include it (like Stripe), fall back to email-based lead matching.
Validate the payment status — only "paid" or "completed" payments generate commissions. Pending or failed payments are rejected with a security audit log.
Find the effective rate — check for a per-affiliate override first, then fall back to the campaign's default commission value.
Calculate the amount — percentage campaigns multiply the sale amount by the rate; flat-fee campaigns use the rate as a fixed amount.
💡
Commission Status Flow

Commissions follow a strict lifecycle: Pending → Approved → Paid. They can also go to Reversed (if a refund or chargeback happens) or Declined (if the owner rejects them). The "confirmed" status no longer exists — it was removed in favor of "approved". Once a commission is paid, it's linked to a payout batch via batchId.

Stage 4: Commission Approval — "Is This Legitimate?"

Not every commission should be paid automatically. The system has multiple layers of review:

✅

Auto-Approval

If the campaign has autoApproveCommissions enabled and the commission is below the approvalThreshold, it's approved automatically. Small commissions don't need human review.

🔍

Manual Review

Large commissions (above the threshold) or those flagged for fraud require the SaaS Owner to manually review and approve them before they can be paid out.

🚫

Reversal

If a customer requests a refund or files a chargeback, the system automatically reverses the associated commission. The affiliate doesn't get paid for refunded sales.

Stage 5: The Payout — "Getting Paid"

Once commissions are approved, they're grouped into payout batches and the affiliate receives their money. Here's how it works:

1

Generate a payout batch

The SaaS Owner clicks "Generate Payout." The system finds all approved commissions that haven't been paid yet and groups them by affiliate.

2

Create individual payout records

Each affiliate gets one payout record in the batch with their total amount. The system also creates a CSV file that can be used for bank transfers.

3

Mark payouts as paid

The owner processes the payments (via bank transfer, PayPal, etc.) and marks each payout as paid in the system. This can be done individually or for the entire batch at once.

4

Update commissions and notify

All commissions in the batch are updated to Paid status. The affiliate receives an email notification that their payment has been sent.

// convex/payouts.ts — generatePayoutBatch

// Find all approved, unpaid commissions
const commissions = await ctx.db
  .query("commissions")
  .withIndex("by_tenant_and_status",
    (q) => q.eq("tenantId", tenantId)
           .eq("status", "approved")
  )
  .collect();

// Filter out commissions already in a batch
const unpaidCommissions = commissions
  .filter((c) => !c.batchId);

// Group by affiliate and sum amounts
for (const comm of unpaidCommissions) {
  const existing = affiliateCommissions
    .get(comm.affiliateId) ?? { amount: 0, count: 0 };
  existing.amount += comm.amount;
  existing.count += 1;
  affiliateCommissions.set(comm.affiliateId, existing);
}
Find all approved commissions for this tenant — these are commissions that have been reviewed and are ready to be paid.
Filter out already-paid commissions — if a commission already has a batchId, it's been included in a previous payout. Don't pay twice!
Group by affiliate — for each affiliate, sum up all their unpaid commissions. This gives us the total amount each affiliate is owed.

Campaign Types & Commission Structures

Not all affiliate programs pay the same way. Campaigns define the rules of the game — how much affiliates earn, how long cookies last, and whether recurring payments generate ongoing commissions.

Two Ways to Pay Affiliates

Every campaign uses one of two commission types. This is the most fundamental decision a SaaS Owner makes when setting up an affiliate program.

📊 Percentage Commission

The affiliate earns a percentage of each sale. If the sale is ₱5,000 and the rate is 20%, the affiliate earns ₱1,000.

Best for: Products with varying prices. The affiliate's earnings scale with the sale amount.

💵 Flat Fee Commission

The affiliate earns a fixed amount per sale, regardless of the sale price. Every conversion earns exactly ₱500.

Best for: Products with a single price point or when you want predictable commission costs.

// The actual calculation logic

Module 5

Fraud Detection — Keeping the System Honest

When money is involved, some people will try to game the system. This module explains how the platform detects and prevents affiliate fraud — specifically the most common type: self-referral.

What Is Self-Referral Fraud?

Self-referral fraud happens when an affiliate tries to earn a commission on their own purchase. Imagine being a salesperson who gets a commission every time you buy something from your own store — that's exactly what this is.

💡 Think of it like this

It's like a restaurant waiter who rings up their own meals as "customer orders" so they can collect tips from themselves. The restaurant is paying out money for sales that didn't actually come from real customers.

The system detects self-referral by comparing data points between the affiliate and the customer:

📧

Email Match (3 points)

The customer's email matches the affiliate's email. This is the strongest signal — if the buyer and the affiliate share an email, it's almost certainly self-referral.

🌐

IP Match (3 points) or Subnet Match (1 point)

The customer's IP address matches the affiliate's login IP. A subnet match (same network, like same office or home WiFi) is a weaker signal.

💻

Device Fingerprint Match (2 points)

The customer's device fingerprint matches the affiliate's device. This catches people using the same computer for both accounts.

💳

Payment Method Match (2 points)

The customer's payment method (last 4 digits or processor ID) matches the affiliate's payout method. Same credit card = same person.

💡
Weighted Scoring System

The system uses a weighted scoring approach. Each matched indicator contributes points. If the total score reaches 3 or higher, the conversion is flagged as self-referral. This is calibrated for the Philippine/SEA market where shared networks (office WiFi, family devices) are common — a single subnet match alone (1 point) won't trigger a false positive.

How Detection Works in the Code

Self-referral detection runs automatically every time a commission is created. It's built into the createCommissionFromConversionInternal function.

// convex/fraudDetection.ts — detectSelfReferral

// Compare email (case-insensitive)
if (conversion.customerEmail
    && affiliate.email) {
  if (conversion.customerEmail
      .toLowerCase()
      === affiliate.email.toLowerCase()) {
    matchedIndicators.push("email_match");
  }
}

// Compare IP address (exact or /24 subnet)
if (conversion.ipAddress
    && affiliate.lastLoginIp) {
  if (conversion.ipAddress
      === affiliate.lastLoginIp) {
    matchedIndicators.push("ip_match");
  } else if (isSameSubnet(
    conversion.ipAddress,
    affiliate.lastLoginIp, 24
  )) {
    matchedIndicators.push("ip_subnet_match");
  }
}

// Calculate weighted score
const totalScore = matchedIndicators
  .reduce((sum, indicator) =>
    sum + (SIGNAL_WEIGHTS[indicator] ?? 1), 0
  );

const isSelfReferral = totalScore >= 3;
Check email match — compare the customer's email with the affiliate's email, ignoring case. If they match, add "email_match" (3 points).
Check IP match — first check for an exact IP match (3 points). If not exact, check if they're on the same /24 subnet (1 point). This catches shared networks without false-flagging entire offices.
Calculate the score — add up the weights of all matched indicators. Email match alone (3 points) triggers detection. IP subnet + device match (1+2=3) also triggers it.

What Happens When Fraud Is Detected

When self-referral is detected, the system takes several actions automatically:

1

Commission is flagged

The commission gets isSelfReferral: true and fraudIndicators listing which signals matched (e.g., ["email_match", "ip_match"]).

2

Commission stays pending

Even if the campaign has auto-approval enabled, self-referral commissions are forced into "pending" status for manual review.

3

Fraud signal is added to affiliate record

A fraud signal entry is added to the affiliate's fraudSignals array with details about what was detected and when.

4

SaaS Owner is notified

A fraud alert email is scheduled to be sent to the SaaS Owner, informing them of the suspicious activity.

5

Audit trail is logged

A security event is logged to the audit trail with full details for compliance and investigation purposes.

Commission Reversal — "Taking Back the Money"

Sometimes a commission needs to be reversed after it's been created. This happens in two scenarios:

↩️

Refund

The customer requested and received a refund. The commission for that sale must be reversed since the sale no longer exists.

🏦

Chargeback

The customer disputed the charge with their bank. The payment was forcibly reversed, and the commission must be reversed too. Chargebacks also add a fraud signal.

🚨
Only Reversible Commissions

Only commissions with status Pending or Approved can be reversed. Once a commission is Paid, it's already been sent to the affiliate and can't be automatically reversed — that would require a separate recovery process.

✅ Exactly! The system is calibrated for the PH/SEA market where shared networks are common. A subnet match alone scores only 1 point, well below the threshold of 3. This prevents false positives when multiple people share the same office or café WiFi.

Why atomic deduplication matters

Payment providers sometimes send the same webhook twice (retries). The system uses a by_event_id unique index to ensure each event is processed exactly once — the check and insert happen in a single database transaction. This is called idempotency, and it's critical for financial systems where processing the same payment twice would mean paying the affiliate twice.

Dual Payment Provider Support

The system supports two payment providers simultaneously: SaligPay (internal) and Stripe Connect. Both normalize their events into a shared BillingEvent format, so the commission engine doesn't need to know which provider sent the event.

💳

SaligPay (Internal)

Webhook events include affiliate metadata directly in the payload (_affilio_ref, _salig_aff_tenant). Attribution is straightforward — read the metadata, find the affiliate, create the commission.

Attribution: Direct metadata in webhook payload

🟣

Stripe Connect

Webhook events do NOT include affiliate metadata. Instead, the system resolves the tenant by Stripe account ID, verifies the webhook signature with the tenant's signing secret, and falls back to email-based lead matching for attribution.

Attribution: Email-based lead matching via referralLeads table

The commission engine doesn't change at all. This is good architecture — the parts that are likely to change (payment providers) are isolated from the parts that are stable (commission logic).

Key Convex Patterns Used Throughout

Understanding these patterns will help you read and modify any file in this codebase:

🔍

Queries (read data)

Use query to read data from the database. They're reactive — when data changes, the UI updates automatically. Always use indexes for efficient reads.

✏️

Mutations (write data)

Use mutation to create, update, or delete data. They run in transactions — either everything succeeds or everything fails. No partial updates.

🌐

Actions (external calls)

Use action to call external APIs (like sending emails or processing webhooks). They run in Node.js, not in the database runtime, so they can use any npm package.

🔒

Internal Functions

Use internalQuery, internalMutation, and internalAction for functions that should only be called by other Convex functions, not directly from the frontend.

The standard function pattern used everywhere 
// Every Convex function follows this pattern:

export const myFunction = query({
